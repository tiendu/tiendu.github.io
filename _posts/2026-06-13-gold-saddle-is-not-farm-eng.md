title: "A Golden Saddle Is Still Not the Farm"
date: 2026-06-13
categories: ["Personal Notes & Reflections"]
---

I saw a headline today about someone becoming one of the richest human beings in history.

I do not really want to make this about his name.

What stayed with me was the scale.

A number so large it barely feels like money anymore.

At some point, money stops looking like rent, food, medicine, school fees, debt, or years of work. It becomes weather. It becomes mythology. It becomes something people point at from far away and pretend to understand.

And today is Saturday.

I am on call.

Tomorrow too.

So while the internet was trying to process a headline about historic wealth, I was also thinking about alerts, weekend coverage, Monday first response, and the strange feeling of being available when the week is supposed to belong to me.

That contrast stayed with me.

Not because my life is tragic.

It is not.

I earn enough to make life a bit easier than it is for many people around me.

Not rich.

Not free.

Not immune from anxiety.

But still, easier.

I know that.

I sit at a computer.

I work with software, systems, tickets, customers, workflows, and platforms.

My work can be stressful, but I do not want to pretend it is the same kind of hardship as a cleaner's work, a factory worker's work, a farmer's work, or a fisherman's work.

It is not.

I do not work physically harder than them.

Most days, I probably do not work harder than them at all.

And yet I earn more than many people who use their bodies much more than I do.

That fact humbles me.

Not because I want to degrade my own work.

My work has value.

Skill matters.

Experience matters.

Responsibility matters.

But I cannot honestly say that everything I have is purely because I worked harder.

Part of it was luck.

Maybe a large part.

Luck of education.

Luck of timing.

Luck of language.

Luck of being able to move.

Luck of working with computers instead of carrying things under the sun.

Luck of entering a field where the market pays better.

Luck of having a path that, even when messy, still gave me some options.

I do not say this to perform humility.

I say it because it feels dishonest not to say it.

There is a French saying I have been thinking about:

> Si en travaillant beaucoup on devenait riche, les ânes auraient le bât doré.

If working a lot made you rich, the donkeys would have golden saddles.

It sounds funny at first.

A little bitter.

A little too cynical.

But on a day like this, it does not sound cynical.

It sounds accurate.

Because if hard work alone made people rich, the world would look very different.

Farmers in Vietnam would be rich.

Fishermen would be rich.

Construction workers would be rich.

Factory workers would be rich.

Street vendors who wake up before sunrise and stand there until night would be rich.

The people who carry, clean, lift, cook, drive, repair, and endure would be the ones at the top.

But they are not.

And everybody knows this.

That is the strange part.

Everybody knows hard work does not automatically lead to wealth.

But we still talk as if it does.

We still tell people to work harder.

Wake up earlier.

Sleep less.

Want it more.

Sacrifice more.

Push more.

As if effort is a magic substance.

As if the only reason people remain poor, tired, or stuck is that they have not poured enough of themselves into the machine yet.

I do not believe that anymore.

Not fully.

I still believe in effort.

I still believe in discipline.

I still believe in doing difficult things when they need to be done.

But I no longer believe effort is automatically noble.

And I no longer believe hard work always compounds.

Sometimes it does.

Sometimes effort becomes skill.

Sometimes it becomes money.

Sometimes it becomes reputation.

Sometimes it becomes ownership.

Sometimes it becomes freedom.

Sometimes it becomes the next door opening.

But sometimes effort just disappears.

Like water poured into a drain.

You still carry the bucket.

You still get tired.

You still lose time.

But nothing stays with you.

Not all hard work accumulates.

Some hard work only proves that you can be used for longer.

I think about farmers in Vietnam.

Not in some romantic way.

There is nothing romantic about a difficult life when you are the one living it.

The sun is still hot.

The debt is still real.

The crop still depends on weather, disease, market price, middlemen, fuel, fertilizer, and luck.

You can wake up early.

You can work all day.

You can do everything right.

Then the price drops.

Or the rain comes wrong.

Or the buyers squeeze you.

Or disease spreads.

Or the cost of everything goes up.

And suddenly your hard work is not a heroic story.

It is just risk.

Fishermen are the same.

The sea does not care how disciplined you are.

Fuel costs money.

Boats cost money.

Equipment breaks.

Storms come.

The catch is uncertain.

The body pays first, long before the reward becomes clear.

So when someone says, "You just need to work harder," I always wonder:

Harder than who?

Harder than the farmer under the sun?

Harder than the fisherman before dawn?

Harder than the woman selling food on the sidewalk from morning until night?

Harder than the worker whose body is already being spent?

The problem is not that people do not work hard.

Many people work brutally hard.

The problem is where the effort goes.

That is why stories about 100-hour work weeks stayed in my head for a long time.

I remember hearing them and thinking about them again and again.

Why?

Just why?

Part of me admired them.

I think many people do.

There is something seductive about the image of someone working all the time, sleeping on the floor, pushing through exhaustion, building something huge.

It makes effort look clean.

Almost sacred.

As if the equation is simple.

Suffer enough, and the future will pay you back.

Endure enough, and you become exceptional.

Work enough, and reality bends.

But that story hides something important.

A founder working insane hours is not the same as an ordinary employee working insane hours.

A founder may be building ownership.

Equity.

Control.

Brand.

Power.

Upside.

The company grows, and the owner's stake grows with it.

The suffering may still be real.

The exhaustion may still be real.

But the structure is different.

When the owner works more, the owner may capture more of the result.

When the worker works more, the result may simply disappear into someone else's machine.

That difference matters.

It matters more than the hours.

A donkey can work all day.

But the donkey does not own the farm.

Maybe the saddle becomes nicer.

Maybe the load becomes bigger.

Maybe people praise the donkey for being strong.

But the farm still belongs to someone else.

That is the part I cannot unsee.

And this weekend, I feel it in a smaller way.

Once a month, I have to stay on call during the weekend.

Saturday and Sunday.

Every Monday, I am first response.

Holiday or not.

If it is my turn, it is my turn.

And the benefit?

More days off.

That is it.

Which is not nothing.

A day off is still a day off.

But it does not answer the deeper question.

After giving more of myself, what actually stays with me?

That question bothers me more than the schedule itself.

Because some work leaves something behind.

A skill.

A relationship.

A reputation.

A tool.

A better bargaining position.

A clearer mind.

A little more freedom.

But some work only leaves fatigue.

You were available.

You responded.

You absorbed the stress.

You carried the responsibility.

Then the week resets.

The customer is answered.

The ticket is closed.

The system continues.

And you are just a little more tired.

That is not the same as growth.

That is not the same as building.

That is not the same as compounding.

It is just leakage.

I do not say this because I want to become lazy.

I say it because I am tired of people treating exhaustion as proof of virtue.

Hard work is not fake.

But hard work is not magic either.

It needs a direction.

It needs a container.

It needs a place where it can accumulate.

Otherwise, effort becomes a ritual.

You keep sacrificing because sacrifice feels responsible.

You keep enduring because endurance feels mature.

You keep saying yes because saying no feels dangerous.

You keep telling yourself that later, somehow, the reward will come.

Sometimes it does.

Sometimes the hard season is necessary.

Sometimes a skill only becomes useful after hundreds of boring repetitions.

Sometimes a better life requires a period of discomfort.

I do not want to deny that.

But sometimes the reward does not come.

Sometimes the hard period is not a bridge.

It is just a pit.

Sometimes the system is not training you.

It is consuming you.

Sometimes extra responsibility does not make you more valuable.

It only makes you more available.

This is why "work harder" is such incomplete advice.

Work harder at what?

For whom?

Under what structure?

With what upside?

What do you keep after the work is done?

If the answer is skill, leverage, trust, ownership, reputation, or a way out, then maybe the effort matters.

But if the answer is only more work, more availability, more unpaid responsibility, and more exhaustion, then maybe the effort is not investment.

Maybe it is drainage.

That is what the old saying gets right.

The donkey works.

The donkey carries.

The donkey endures.

Maybe the donkey even becomes proud of how much it can carry.

But the donkey does not become rich just because it suffers well.

And that is painful to admit because many of us were raised on a more comforting story.

Study hard.

Work hard.

Be useful.

Be patient.

Do not complain.

The reward will come.

It is not a completely false story.

But it is not complete either.

Hard work is one part.

Structure is another.

Ownership is another.

Timing is another.

Luck is another.

Class is another.

Market power is another.

Geography is another.

Health is another.

Family is another.

Who captures the value is another.

That last one may be the most important.

Who captures the value?

The farmer grows the food.

But who controls the price?

The fisherman brings back the catch.

But who controls the market?

The cleaner keeps the room usable.

But who gets respect?

The worker keeps the system running.

But who owns the system?

The employee stays on call.

But who benefits from that availability?

The question is not only how hard someone works.

The question is whether the result of that work returns to them.

If it does not, then hard work becomes a moral decoration placed on top of extraction.

People praise your discipline while taking your time.

People praise your resilience while giving you more to endure.

People praise your ownership mindset while giving you no ownership.

That one is especially funny.

Ownership mindset.

A phrase for people who are asked to care like owners while being paid like employees.

I used to find that phrase inspiring.

Now I find it suspicious.

Because if you want people to think like owners, maybe give them some ownership.

Otherwise, just say the truth.

You want people to carry the anxiety of ownership without the reward.

That is not leadership.

That is extraction with nicer vocabulary.

Still, I do not want this to become a simple anti-work rant.

Work matters.

Effort matters.

Discipline matters.

There is dignity in doing something well.

There is dignity in showing up.

There is dignity in being reliable.

There is dignity in doing difficult work that others depend on.

The farmer is not foolish for working.

The fisherman is not foolish for going to sea.

The cleaner is not foolish for cleaning.

The worker is not foolish for trying.

A person staying on call is not foolish for being responsible.

The foolish part is believing that effort alone guarantees justice.

It does not.

Effort deserves respect.

But effort also deserves a good destination.

That is what I am trying to learn.

Not to stop working.

Not to stop trying.

Not to become cynical in a cheap way.

But to ask better questions before giving more of myself.

Is this building skill?

Is this building leverage?

Is this building freedom?

Is this building something I can keep?

Or am I just becoming a better donkey?

A stronger donkey.

A more reliable donkey.

A donkey with a slightly nicer saddle.

Maybe even a golden one.

But still not the owner of the farm.

That is the uncomfortable question.

And maybe it is the question everyone should ask before worshipping extreme work weeks.

Not:

Can I endure this?

People can endure many things.

The better question is:

What does this endurance become?

Does it become a bridge?

Or does it become a drain?

Because if effort goes into the wrong place, it does not matter how sincere it was.

It still disappears.

And you are left standing there, tired, disciplined, praised maybe, but no freer than before.

That is not laziness talking.

That is just accounting.

A life has only so much time.

A body has only so much energy.

A person has only so many weekends to give away.

So yes, work hard when the work deserves it.

Work hard when it builds something.

Work hard when it teaches you.

Work hard when it gives you options.

Work hard when it helps someone you love.

Work hard when the sacrifice has a place to land.

But do not worship work just because it hurts.

Pain is not proof of progress.

Exhaustion is not proof of meaning.

And a golden saddle is still not the farm.
