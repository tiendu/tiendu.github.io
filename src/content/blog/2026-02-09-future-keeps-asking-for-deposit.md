---
title: "The Future Keeps Asking for a Deposit"
date: 2026-02-09
categories: ["Personal Notes & Reflections"]
---

I found a video about doing nothing while doomscrolling.

Which is probably the most modern way to fail at the thing you are trying to understand.

I was not really watching anything. Not properly.

Just moving my thumb, letting one thing become another thing, one thought become another thought, one face become another face. Feeding the machine enough attention so it would keep giving me something back.

Then the video started talking about stillness.

Doing nothing.

Not reading.

Not scrolling.

Not meditating.

Not even resting in a productive way.

Just sitting there.

Existing.

That sounds simple until you actually try it.

The moment I stop doing something, another part of me starts asking questions.

Shouldn't you be learning?

Shouldn't you be applying?

Shouldn't you be improving your CV?

Shouldn't you be fixing your website?

Shouldn't you be writing?

Shouldn't you be preparing for the next interview?

Shouldn't you be doing something that makes the future less uncertain?

The annoying thing is that none of those questions are completely wrong.

That is what makes them difficult.

I am looking for a new job.

I want higher pay.

I want a less toxic environment.

I want work that does not make me feel smaller every week.

I want to move toward something better, or at least something less exhausting than the current shape of things.

So I cannot simply sit in a chair and pretend ambition is fake.

Money matters.

Environment matters.

Career direction matters.

Learning matters.

A better job can change your daily life in very real ways. It can change the amount of dread you feel on Sunday night. It can change how much room you have in your head after work. It can change whether your body feels tight every morning before opening the laptop.

So yes, I want change.

But change has its own fear.

What if the next employer is the same thing with a different logo?

What if the new environment is only better during the interview?

What if the nice words are just another layer of company paint?

What if the new team has the same vague expectations, the same politics, the same hidden toxicity?

What if I learn a new skill and it helps?

What if I learn a new skill and it does not?

What if doing things I do not like is the entrance fee for a better future?

What if doing things I do not like is just another way to lose more time?

That is the promise I keep running into.

Endure now.

Learn now.

Sacrifice now.

Stay patient now.

Take the bad period now.

Do the thing you dislike now.

Later, the reward will come.

Maybe it will.

Maybe it will not.

But the future keeps asking for a deposit.

That is the part I have been thinking about.

Not ambition itself.

I still want to get better.

I still want to build skills.

I still want to earn more.

I still want a life with more room in it.

But I am tired of the feeling that the present is never allowed to be enough for even five minutes.

The present is always treated like raw material.

Something to invest.

Something to optimize.

Something to convert into a better future version of myself.

Sleep is for recovery.

Exercise is for performance.

Reading is for leverage.

Writing is for personal brand.

Learning is for employability.

Rest is for coming back stronger.

Even doing nothing becomes something you do so you can do more later.

At some point, life starts to feel like it is always being spent in advance.

That is probably why the video stayed with me.

It mentioned *Perfect Days*.

The film follows a man in Tokyo named Hirayama.

He cleans public toilets.

That is his job.

Not as a hidden starting point before he becomes someone else.

Not as the humiliating first act before the movie rewards him with a better life.

He just cleans toilets.

And the film takes that seriously.

He wakes up early.

He folds his things.

He looks after his small plants.

He buys canned coffee from a vending machine.

He gets into his van and listens to old cassette tapes.

Then he drives through Tokyo and cleans the toilets carefully.

Not lazily.

Not resentfully.

Carefully.

He checks corners.

He wipes surfaces.

He notices light.

He eats lunch in a park and takes photos of trees with a small camera.

Not dramatic trees.

Just branches, leaves, shadows, and sunlight filtering through them.

Komorebi.

木漏れ日.

Then the day continues.

A public bath.

A simple meal.

A paperback book before sleep.

Then sleep.

Then another morning.

Then the same thing again.

The film is not rushing to rescue him from this routine.

That is what makes it strange.

There is no big escape.

No hidden genius reveal.

No moment where the world suddenly discovers him.

No dramatic proof that his quiet life was secretly leading to something spectacular.

There are just days.

Repeated days.

A cassette tape.

A toilet cleaned carefully.

A book before sleep.

Light through trees.

At first, the instinct is to feel sorry for him.

Or at least to ask the question we have been trained to ask.

Is this it?

Shouldn't he want more?

Shouldn't he be building something?

Shouldn't he be moving up?

Shouldn't he be converting this life into a better one?

Shouldn't there be a plan?

That instinct says a lot.

Because it is not really about him.

It is about us.

It is about how uncomfortable we have become with a life that does not advertise its own progress.

A quiet routine now looks like surrender.

A simple job looks like failure.

A person who does not visibly chase more can seem suspicious, almost irresponsible.

We look at stillness and call it lack of ambition.

We look at contentment and call it giving up.

But I do not want to romanticize it either.

Cleaning toilets is work.

Work is still work.

Money still matters.

Rent still exists.

Family still gets sick.

A peaceful routine does not pay every bill by itself.

A book and a tree cannot solve a toxic workplace.

And if someone is underpaid, exhausted, or trapped in a bad environment, no amount of poetic stillness should be used to shame them for wanting something better.

That would be dishonest.

The difficult thing is holding both truths at the same time.

Wanting better is valid.

But postponing your whole life until better arrives can become its own trap.

I know this because I keep doing it.

When I get a better job, then I will relax.

When I earn more, then I will feel safer.

When I leave this environment, then I will become lighter.

When I learn the right skill, then I will be more confident.

When the next chapter starts, then maybe I can finally feel like I am living correctly.

The sentence keeps changing, but the structure is the same.

When this happens, then I can live.

The future becomes a creditor.

And every day, it collects.

A little attention.

A little peace.

A little sleep.

A little dignity.

A little willingness to do things you do not like because maybe they will become useful later.

Sometimes that bargain is necessary.

Sometimes it is even wise.

You endure a difficult period because there really is something on the other side.

You learn the skill.

You apply for the job.

You save money.

You leave the bad room.

You do the boring work that gives your future more options.

That is real.

But not every sacrifice matures into meaning.

Not every unpleasant thing becomes a seed.

Some things are just unpleasant.

Some jobs are just bad.

Some environments are just toxic.

Some skills are learned out of fear and forgotten out of exhaustion.

Some promises never pay back.

That is the part nobody wants to say too loudly because the whole culture depends on the opposite belief.

Endure now.

Rewards are there.

Keep going.

Trust the process.

Pain is growth.

Discomfort is expansion.

Everything difficult is secretly preparation.

Maybe.

Sometimes.

But not always.

Sometimes pain is just pain.

Sometimes a bad environment does not build character.

It just drains you.

Sometimes doing what you hate does not lead to a better life.

It just teaches you to tolerate hating your life.

That is the line I am trying to see more clearly.

Because I still need to work.

I still need to learn.

I still need to participate.

I still need to be practical.

I cannot opt out of the world just because I watched a quiet movie about a toilet cleaner in Tokyo.

But maybe I can stop believing that every minute must justify itself to the future.

Maybe I can apply for jobs and still sit still sometimes.

Maybe I can learn a skill without turning my whole identity into a rescue mission.

Maybe I can want higher pay without letting money become the only measure of whether my life is improving.

Maybe I can leave a toxic environment without imagining that the next place will save me completely.

Maybe I can admit that the unknown is still unknown.

That sounds obvious, but it is hard.

When you are unhappy, the next thing becomes very powerful.

The next job.

The next salary.

The next skill.

The next version of yourself.

The next room where maybe everything finally feels easier.

And sometimes the next thing does help.

But it is still a room.

It still has walls.

It still has people.

It still has trade-offs.

It still has ordinary days.

It still requires you to live inside it after the excitement fades.

That is what *Perfect Days* quietly shows.

Not that small lives are better than ambitious lives.

Not that simple jobs are spiritually superior.

Not that everyone should clean toilets and take photos of trees.

The film is not naive about sadness either.

Hirayama is peaceful, but he is not empty.

There are hints of distance.

Family history.

Loneliness.

A niece who briefly enters his life.

A sister who belongs to a different world.

A past that is never fully explained.

That matters.

Because the film is not saying that simplicity erases pain.

It is saying something quieter.

A life can contain pain and still not need to turn itself into a performance.

A person can have history and still wash the next surface.

A person can be lonely and still notice light through trees.

A person can be ordinary and still be awake inside the day.

I find that difficult.

Maybe most people do.

Because if a day is just a day, then we have to actually be inside it.

And being inside the day can feel uncomfortable when the future is knocking on the door asking for more.

More effort.

More proof.

More sacrifice.

More improvement.

More willingness to become someone else.

I think this is why doing nothing feels so strange now.

It is not because nothing is hard.

It is because nothing does not produce a receipt.

No output.

No metric.

No lesson.

No progress bar.

You just sat there.

You looked out the window.

You let the room be quiet.

You did not convert the moment into anything.

And some part of you panicked.

What if this is wasting time?

What if I fall behind?

What if someone else is learning while I am sitting here?

What if someone else is applying while I am breathing?

What if someone else is becoming better while I am doing nothing?

That fear is not imaginary.

Other people are moving.

The world is competitive.

Jobs are not guaranteed.

Rent does not care about your philosophical relationship with stillness.

But maybe the answer is not to turn the whole self into a machine of preparation.

Maybe the answer is smaller.

Less impressive.

Almost boring.

To keep moving, but not worship movement.

To keep learning, but not become possessed by improvement.

To look for a better job, but not hand your entire soul to the job search.

To want more money, but not treat your current life as worthless until the number changes.

To rest without making rest another performance.

To sometimes let five minutes be five minutes.

No phone.

No podcast.

No plan.

No self-improvement.

No explanation.

Just five minutes where the future does not get to collect anything.

That sounds almost stupidly small.

Maybe it is.

But small things matter when the whole system is built to take them away.

Two minutes waiting for coffee without reaching for the phone.

A walk with no productivity attached.

A meal eaten without turning it into a task.

A quiet evening that does not need to become content.

A page of a book read slowly.

A song listened to without doing something else.

A tree looked at for no reason.

The system has no use for that.

Which is probably why it feels useful.

I do not know if I can live like Hirayama.

Probably not.

I am not that calm.

I still worry too much.

I still compare.

I still want a better career.

I still want the next thing to work.

I still want the future to reward the deposits I have already made.

But maybe the point is not to become him.

Maybe the point is to notice why his life feels so strange.

A man cleaning toilets, reading books, taking photos of trees, and not trying to turn himself into an inspirational brand.

A man whose life does not seem to be waiting for applause.

A man who is not asking the future to justify every day.

Maybe that is what unsettles me.

Not the toilet cleaning.

The absence of pleading.

His life is not begging to be recognized as meaningful.

It simply is.

I do not know how to get there.

Maybe I do not need to get there fully.

Maybe it is enough to borrow a little of that feeling.

Just enough to remember that I am allowed to live some parts of my life without turning them into strategy.

Not all of it.

Applications need strategy.

Money needs strategy.

Career changes need strategy.

Leaving bad environments needs strategy.

But not everything.

Some parts of life need to remain unoptimized, or there will be nothing left that feels like life.

That is what I want to keep.

Even while looking for a new job.

Even while learning.

Even while worrying.

Even while doing things I do not like because they may help later.

I want some part of me to remain outside the bargain.

A part that can sit still without asking what it is worth.

A part that can listen to a song without turning it into motivation.

A part that can look at a tree and not need a lesson from it.

A part that can say:

This moment does not have to become anything.

It can just be here.

Maybe tomorrow I will apply again.

Maybe tomorrow I will learn something useful.

Maybe tomorrow I will worry about employers, salaries, interviews, skills, and whether the next room is actually better than the current one.

But for five minutes, maybe I can stop paying the deposit.

The future can wait outside.

Not forever.

Just for five minutes.

And maybe five minutes is not freedom.

But it is not nothing either.
